Predicting Colorectal Cancer Survival Using SEER Data: A Regression-Based Analysis

📊 Dataset: SEER Program 📋 Records: 198,346 📅 Diagnosis Years: 2010–2015 🔬 Model: Multiple Linear Regression

Abstract

This study analyzes colorectal cancer survival outcomes using a dataset derived from the Surveillance, Epidemiology, and End Results (SEER) program. The objective is to evaluate whether patient demographics and clinical characteristics can predict survival duration following diagnosis. A multiple linear regression model was developed using features such as cancer stage, age, race, and diagnosis year. Results indicate that cancer stage is the strongest predictor of survival, with localized disease associated with significantly improved outcomes. While the model demonstrates moderate predictive performance (R² = 0.3114), findings highlight the value of structured healthcare data in identifying survival patterns and informing data-driven decision-making in oncology.

01 — Introduction

Introduction

This project examines colorectal cancer survival outcomes using a cleaned dataset derived from the SEER program. The primary objective is to assess whether patient demographics and clinical characteristics can be used to predict survival duration following a colorectal cancer diagnosis.

Survival analysis is a core component of healthcare analytics, particularly in oncology. Identifying patient populations at higher risk for poor outcomes enables providers to implement earlier interventions, enhance care coordination, and allocate resources more effectively. By analyzing patterns across variables such as cancer stage, age, race, and year of diagnosis, this study aims to identify the factors most strongly associated with survival duration and support data-driven decision-making in cancer care.

02 — Data

Data Collection

The dataset used in this analysis originates from the SEER program, a nationally recognized cancer surveillance system that collects detailed information on cancer incidence, patient demographics, tumor characteristics, treatment, and survival outcomes. SEER was selected due to its comprehensive scope, standardized structure, and widespread use in oncology research.

An R² value of 0.3114 indicates that the model explains approximately 31% of the variance in survival outcomes — moderate but expected performance given the clinical complexity of cancer survival and the limited feature set available. Survival outcomes are influenced by many factors not captured here (treatment type, comorbidities, tumor grade), so this level of explanatory power is reasonable for a regression-based baseline model. Model Interpretation

Model coefficients indicated that cancer stage, age, and diagnosis year were the most influential predictors. Note that linear regression was used for pedagogical simplicity; survival-specific models such as Cox proportional hazards or Kaplan-Meier estimators would better handle censored data and could improve accuracy.

05 — Key Findings

Key Findings

  • Cancer stage is the strongest predictor of survival (Localized: +30.4 mo.; Distant: reference)
  • Localized disease is associated with significantly longer survival compared to distant-stage diagnosis
  • Age ≥85 is consistently linked to poorer outcomes (−11.6 months on average)
  • Racial disparities are statistically present but clinically modest (effect sizes <2 months)
  • The negative association for diagnosis year reflects right-censoring bias, not worsening outcomes over time
  • R² = 0.31 represents moderate but expected performance given the limited feature set and clinical complexity of survival prediction
06 — Conclusion

Conclusion

This analysis used SEER colorectal cancer data to model survival outcomes based on patient demographics and clinical characteristics. Results indicate that cancer stage at diagnosis is the most significant predictor of survival, with localized disease associated with substantially better outcomes. Age also plays a critical role, with older patients experiencing shorter survival durations.

From a healthcare perspective, these findings reinforce the importance of early detection and timely intervention. Data-driven insights such as these can support targeted screening efforts, improve care coordination, and guide resource allocation for high-risk populations.

Potential Applications

Expanding colorectal cancer screening programs
Enhancing follow-up care for advanced-stage patients

Overall, this project demonstrates the value of structured healthcare data in uncovering meaningful patterns and supporting informed decision-making in oncology care.
